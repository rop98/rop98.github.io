How it works

Explanation:

Light comes through lens pass through a hole in back of lens (aperture) you can change the size

Aperture Affects Depth of Field = amount of stuff thats nice and sharp

  • Small shallow depth of field means small space is focused so the background and the front would be blurry

  • Big deep depth of field means everything is in focus

    TIP - Portrait photography, depth of field focus on subject = more professional, looks 3D

    TIP - Landscape photography - Big / deep depth of field for all the detail in the image as nearly everything is the subject

Shutter Speed affects movement

  • Fast shutter speed for fast movement when you want detail

  • Slow Shutter speed is when the picture has a chance to movement blur. e.g waterfalls Can be used to add a bit of life

Physical Buttons

  • Zoom twist barrel
  • af/mf switch for auto and manual focus
  • stabilizer switch for optical statbilization usually keep it on

Shutter is two stage button

  • half to focus
  • full to shoot

Supporting the camera

  • hold steady by grip and hold closer to the lens and support the camera

  • palm inwards

  • elbows in to keep steady ( brace )

  • Vertical shot grip on top and elbow on chest

  • Squeeze don’t press shoot hard

Setting the AF Mode:

  • Single Focus (one shot / af-s)

  • Tracking Focus:

    • AI-SERVO / AF-C
    • So long as you’re holding the button down it will continue to focus

Setting AF Points:

  • autofocus points

By default all points are on in AUTO MODE Will find the first thing with contrast

Usually set the centre as the focus point

  • AF-Area Mode in Nikon
  • Canon Has a button on the corner

Set diopter this is the viewfinder, if things are blurry adjust this

Horizontal Horizons, so not every little photo is wonky.

You can lose data and have pixels be smeared when adjusting it back to level

Look for a horizon line and have the viewfinder be parallel with it

Also for Vertical horizons check that the building is to the side of the viewfinder

Rule of thirds

Give subject room to move into

Shots with the subject in the intersection of the rules look balanced and if there is a subject showing movement then a subject coming from right to left and would be better captured in the upper right or lower right to demonstrate the distance left in the journey

Putting the key point of the subject in the intersection (the eye) will make it look better as its more powerful

Tip: Focus on subject and then take picture, focus on its eyes

Reflection photos keys on the intersection

GOOD COMPOSITION BEATS Trying to “fit everything in”

This means that sometimes framing it up is more important than getting all the interesting parts into a shot

People don’t know what they’re missing if you don’t include it in the photo

Framing

Deliberately including something up close in your photo in order to give perspective and something to contain the image instead of letting it fall off.

You might run the risk of having a boring image if there’s nothing to give it context it might be flat.

It also pulls our attention back to the middle of the photo

Tip: if shot is far off, find a tuft of grass and take the shot from there to frame the shot

Using a tree to frame off half the shot, it makes it more 3D pulls our attention to the middle

This can also be done with the top and bottom of shots

Check your backgrounds

Checklist: what is in the shot.

  • Adjust to change the shot if the background blends too much with the subject or if there is an obstruction.
  • You can get down lower and change the background. Think about clean contrast.
  • Get higher or further away or lower.

Ant on a green stick grey background move to have leaf as background

Fill the frame

  • Crop it in tighter
  • Focus on the most important part, it will make for a much better image
  • Find the focal points
  • Don’t always try to get everything into a shot. Make it tell a story. A simple story.
  • Too many things can be distracting
  • Find out what is interesting, what caught my attention. You don’t have to explicitly have the full subject.
  • Enough information to figure out the story

Leading lines

  • Guide your eye, link things up with a line
  • Make sure the line comes in from the corner of the shot.

Anything but Eye-Level

  • Else everything always just looks the same
  • Add an angle of interest on a boring subject
  • Think of penguin from below and from above
  • If not trying to do creative angle, at least get to same level as subject
  • Get down to dog height, get down to gecko Height. Focus on eye and get it in intersection

Look for details

  • See how little leaves have beautiful curls on the end of them
  • How leaves have little droplets
  • Interesting fibres
  • Patterns
  • Reflections in eye

Don’t forget to take vertical shots, always take a horizontal and vertical shot.

  • Even for landscape shots. The perspective might change completely

Exposure is light falling onto image sensor

Entire time shutter is open more and more light is collecting = brighter Camera will choose to make sure that it is mid brightness.

This is contrast limitation. Can’t see into the bright and the dark at the same time. If there are two harsh darks or too harsh lights then it will be underexposed or overexposed as it will not be able to both lighten and darken unless you’re using high dynamic range.

Focus and expose something that is too light and then everything will be washed out. Some bits are way too bright and some are way too dark. Cameras can’t cope well with scenes that differ too much in brightness

  • Underexposed: Too little light, dark and washed out
  • Overexposed: Too much light, bright mess and washed out
  • Correct: Just the right amount of light

Collecting the right amount of light

Small aperture long shutter speed for right exposure

Large aperture short shutter speed for right exposure

Using the right camera mode

P - for program mode, a step above auto mode

Suggests camera aperture same as in auto but you can scroll away from those defaults

Shutter speed : 1/125 is 1 hundred and a 25th of a second

F4.5 : Size of aperture

camera sets levels based on calculating mid-brightness Determining which part will come out mid brightness

Metering Modes:

Whole picture overall average Canon: Evaluative (Nikon :matrix)

Partial Metering: Light meter looks at mid region and make that mid brightness

Spot Metering: point at one spot and make that part the basis for mid brightness

Exposure Compensation

+- Brightness buton

-2..1..^..1..+2

-2 darker collect less light good for sillhouette shots and a sunset

^ mid brightness collect more light

+2 brighter
